> On the animation choices: motion earned its place here only where it mirrors something the subject actually
 does — the step sequencer's traveling light and the waveform bars animate constantly because a synth is never static,
 so a hero that sat still would misrepresent the product. Hover states on knobs, cards, and buttons use short,
 physical transitions (rotate, lift, glow) because a musician's hands are always adjusting something, and instant 
snaps or long lingering fades would both feel wrong for that kind of tactile feedback. The features section reveals 
on scroll rather than on load, because doing it upfront would compete with the hero for attention and the stagger reads
 as three modules "powering on" in sequence, echoing the rack metaphor. I deliberately avoided animating body text, 
the nav, or anything unrelated to signal flow — motion on every element is what makes a page feel restless rather than
 alive, so anything that didn't have a reason tied to the subject was left still.

The short version of the rule I used: animate something only if it mirrors a real behavior of the subject
 (a synth that's always moving) or gives physical feedback to a direct interaction (a hover, a click).
 Everything else — text, layout, nav — stayed still on purpose, since a page where every element moves ends up 
reading as noisy rather than alive.
